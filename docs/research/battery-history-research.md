# Battery History Research

Reference document for the battery timeline on the "How Batteries Work" page.

## Ancient & Early Discoveries

### ~600 BCE -- Static Electricity (Thales of Miletus)
- Rubbing amber with fur attracted lightweight objects (feathers, straw)
- Greek word for amber: *elektron* -- origin of "electricity"
- No practical follow-up for over 2,000 years; treated as a curiosity

### ~250 BCE -- The Baghdad Battery
- Small clay jar found near Baghdad containing copper cylinder and iron rod
- Could theoretically produce ~1V if filled with acidic liquid (vinegar)
- Discovered by archaeologists in 1938
- **Status: disputed.** Most mainstream archaeologists believe it stored scrolls or had ritual purpose
- Mythbusters tested replicas: produced voltage, but barely enough to tingle a tongue
- Should be presented as "mysterious" not "first battery"

### 1745 -- Leyden Jar (Pieter van Musschenbroek)
- Glass jar lined with metal foil; first device to store electrical charge (first capacitor)
- Independently invented by Ewald Georg von Kleist
- Famous demonstration by Abbe Nollet: discharged through 200 monks holding hands; all jumped simultaneously
- Proved electricity could be captured and stored, not just generated in the moment

### 1780 -- Galvani's Frog Legs (Luigi Galvani)
- Touched metal scalpel to dead frog leg, observed twitching
- Hypothesized "animal electricity" (incorrect explanation)
- Real cause: two different metals (scalpel + brass hook) creating circuit through salty tissue
- His rival Volta figured out the true mechanism, leading to the voltaic pile
- Word "galvanize" (shock into action) derives from his name

## 18th-19th Century Breakthroughs

### 1800 -- Voltaic Pile (Alessandro Volta)
- Alternating discs of zinc and copper separated by saltwater-soaked cardboard
- First device to produce **continuous** flow of electricity
- All previous devices (Leyden jar) were one-shot
- Napoleon made Volta a Count
- Unit of electrical potential ("volt") named after him
- Announced via letter to the Royal Society of London

### 1836 -- Daniell Cell (John Frederic Daniell)
- Solved hydrogen bubble problem in Volta's pile
- Two-liquid design (copper sulfate + zinc sulfate) separated by porous barrier
- Steady 1.1V output
- Reliable enough to power telegraph networks
- Became standard for voltage measurement
- Enabled long-distance communication infrastructure

### 1859 -- Lead-Acid Battery (Gaston Plante)
- First **rechargeable** battery: two lead plates in sulfuric acid
- Reversible chemistry -- charge, drain, charge again
- Same basic technology still in car batteries today (165+ years)
- Lead-acid batteries: most recycled consumer product (~99% recycled)

### 1866 -- Zinc-Carbon Cell (Georges Leclanche)
- Zinc rod + manganese dioxide cathode + ammonium chloride paste
- Not rechargeable but cheap, portable, practical
- Direct ancestor of disposable batteries
- Dry cell version patented by Carl Gassner (1886) made batteries truly portable
- Basic chemistry still used in cheapest batteries today

## 20th Century

### 1899 -- Nickel-Cadmium (Waldemar Jungner)
- More rugged than lead-acid, worked in extreme temperatures
- Rechargeable hundreds of times
- Not mass-produced until 1940s-50s
- Powered cordless revolution: power tools, emergency lighting
- "Memory effect" quirk: recharging before full drain reduced capacity
- This limitation drove development of better alternatives

### 1912 -- Gilbert Lewis: Lithium Concept
- Early theoretical work on lithium as electrode material
- Lithium is the lightest metal and has highest electrochemical potential
- Practical implementation wouldn't come for 60+ years

### 1955 -- Alkaline Battery (Lewis Urry, Eveready/Energizer)
- Zinc + manganese dioxide + alkaline (potassium hydroxide) electrolyte
- 5-8x more energy than zinc-carbon cells
- Made portable electronics practical: transistor radios, Walkmans, Game Boys
- Famous demo: toy car race -- alkaline car went full hallway, zinc-carbon barely moved

## The Lithium-Ion Revolution

### 1976 -- Lithium Intercalation (M. Stanley Whittingham)
- Working at Exxon
- First rechargeable lithium battery using titanium disulfide cathode
- Worked but formed dangerous dendrites (fire risk)
- British-American chemist

### 1980 -- LiCoO2 Cathode (John B. Goodenough)
- Working at Oxford University
- Cobalt oxide cathode roughly doubled voltage
- Single most important material discovery in battery history
- Made the discovery at age 57
- Won Nobel Prize at 97 (oldest laureate ever)
- Continued working until shortly before death at 100 (2023)

### 1985 -- Carbon Anode (Akira Yoshino)
- Working at Asahi Kasei (Japan)
- Replaced pure lithium anode with carbon-based material
- Eliminated dendrite fire risk
- Made lithium-ion safe enough to manufacture commercially

### 1991 -- First Commercial Li-ion (Sony)
- Commercialized Yoshino's design in a camcorder battery
- Lighter, higher capacity, longer lasting than anything before
- Within a decade: in every laptop and phone on Earth
- Price trajectory: ~$3,000/kWh (1991) -> below $140/kWh (2024), 95%+ decline
- Direct enabler of the smartphone revolution

## Modern Era

### 2008 -- Tesla Roadster
- Proved lithium-ion could power a real car (0-60 in 3.7s, 244-mile range)
- Changed automotive industry's dismissal of EVs

### 2015 -- Tesla Powerwall
- Wall-mounted home battery for solar storage and backup
- Made home batteries desirable and mainstream
- At unveiling, entire venue was secretly running on Powerwalls (revealed at end)

### 2017 -- Hornsdale Power Reserve
- World's largest Li-ion battery at the time: 100 MW / 129 MWh
- Built in South Australia after catastrophic blackouts
- Elon Musk's "100 days or it's free" bet; finished in ~60 days
- Responded to coal plant trip in 140 milliseconds (before operators noticed)
- Saved ~$40 million in energy costs in first year
- Proof of concept that launched global wave of grid battery construction

### 2019 -- Nobel Prize in Chemistry
- Shared by Goodenough, Whittingham, Yoshino
- "For the development of lithium-ion batteries"
- Committee called them a technology that "created a rechargeable world"

## Debunked Connections

### Leonardo da Vinci
- **No connection to batteries or practical electricity**
- Lived 1452-1519, roughly 280 years before the first battery
- Static electricity as a field didn't begin until William Gilbert's work in 1600
- Pop-history sources occasionally try to connect da Vinci to electricity but these are based on misreadings or fabrication
- Should NOT be included in a factual timeline

## Sources & Confidence Notes
- Baghdad Battery purpose: genuinely disputed among archaeologists; present with appropriate caveats
- Hornsdale savings figure ($40M first year): commonly cited, exact figures vary by source
- Urry toy car demo: reported by Energizer corporate history
- All other entries: high confidence, well-documented historical record
