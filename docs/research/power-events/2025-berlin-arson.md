# Berlin Grid Infrastructure Arson Attacks (2025-2026)

Two coordinated arson attacks on Berlin's high-voltage power grid in September 2025 and January 2026 exposed critical vulnerabilities in urban electricity infrastructure, leaving tens of thousands without power for days and triggering Germany's most severe urban blackouts since World War II.

---

## Event 1: Johannisthal / Adlershof Attack -- September 9, 2025

### What Happened

At approximately **3:30 AM on September 9, 2025**, unknown perpetrators set fire to **two 110-kilovolt overhead line pylons** in the Johannisthal neighborhood of southeast Berlin (Treptow-Kopenick district). These pylons represented the **transition point from overhead lines to underground cables** -- a critical junction in the grid topology. The attackers reportedly wrapped steel chains around the cable insulation and ignited accelerants, causing a massive short circuit that cascaded through the network.

The fires destroyed multiple 110 kV high-voltage cables, severing power to several distribution substations across the southeast.

### Infrastructure Targeted

- **Two 110 kV transmission pylons** at the overhead-to-underground cable transition
- Located near the **Adlershof Technology Park** (WISTA), Berlin's largest tech and science campus
- The Neukolln district heating plant also lost power as a secondary effect

### Impact

| Metric | Value |
|--------|-------|
| Households affected | ~42,000-50,000 |
| Businesses affected | ~3,000 |
| Peak without power | ~50,000 endpoints |
| Duration to full restoration | **~60 hours** (restored Sept 11, 4:37 PM) |

- **By 10:21 AM** on Sept 9: 14,000 customers reconnected
- **By 4:28 PM** on Sept 9: additional 3,000 restored
- **31,000 customers** remained without power through the evening of Sept 9
- Full restoration required extensive civil engineering and cable-pulling work
- **Interim solution**: Stromnetz Berlin established a temporary connection between two lines near the damaged pylons

Affected neighborhoods: Johannisthal, Adlershof, Altglienicke, Bohnsdorf, Niederschoneweide, Grunau, and surrounding districts.

Public transport was paralyzed, traffic lights went dark, schools closed, and mobile police units were deployed.

### Claim of Responsibility

An anonymous communique posted on **Indymedia** (a left-wing media platform) claimed the action, stating it was "an attack on the military-industrial complex" targeting the Adlershof technology park. The group named specific companies: **Atos, Jenoptik, Siemens, and the German Aerospace Centre (DLR)**, accusing them of supplying militaries, enabling border surveillance, and environmental destruction.

The communique stated it was "by no means our intention" to cut residential power -- the goal was to "turn off the juice to the military-industrial complex."

An SPD official noted the attack required **"insider knowledge"**, as the pylons' connection to Adlershof's power supply was not obvious.

### Investigation

Berlin police confirmed arson and the State Security division took over the investigation. Authorities described the attack as "possibly politically motivated."

## Event 2: Lichterfelde / Teltow Canal Attack -- January 3, 2026

### What Happened

At approximately **6:00 AM CET on January 3, 2026**, perpetrators set fire to a **cable bridge spanning the Teltow Canal** near the **Lichterfelde combined heat and power (CHP) plant** in southwest Berlin. The fire destroyed:

- **Five 110 kV high-voltage cables**
- **Ten 10 kV medium-voltage cables**

All damaged cables had to be forcibly switched off, collapsing power supply across multiple southwest Berlin districts.

### Infrastructure Targeted

- **Cable bridge over the Teltow Canal**: an exposed, above-ground crossing carrying critical transmission and distribution cables to the Lichterfelde gas-fired cogeneration plant
- Cable bridges are structurally vulnerable -- they concentrate multiple cable runs in a single accessible location over waterways

### Impact

| Metric | Value |
|--------|-------|
| Households affected | ~45,000 |
| Businesses affected | ~2,200 |
| People affected | ~100,000 |
| Duration to full restoration | **~4-5 days** (restored Jan 7-8, 2026) |

- The longest power outage in Berlin **since 1945**
- Occurred during **freezing winter temperatures** with snow, compounding the crisis
- Thousands lost **heating** alongside electricity (the Lichterfelde CHP plant supplies district heating)
- Hospitals and nursing homes were affected
- Secondary effects included burglaries, train stoppages, and heating outages
- An **emergency shelter** was established at a sports center in Zehlendorf
- Restoration was hampered by adverse weather and the need for fire scene evidence collection

Affected districts: Nikolassee, Zehlendorf, Wannsee, and Lichterfelde.

### Claim of Responsibility: Vulkangruppe

The **Vulkangruppe** (Volcano Group) claimed responsibility via an online letter. The group:

- Framed the attack as targeting **fossil fuel infrastructure** (the Lichterfelde gas-fired plant)
- Described the action as "an act of resistance" and "self-defense and international solidarity"
- Expressed partial regret: *"We apologize to the less wealthy people in southwestern Berlin"*
- Notably, a subsequent statement by a Vulkangruppe faction **distanced itself** from the actions carried out in its name

### Investigation and Government Response

- **January 6, 2026**: Investigation transferred from Berlin police to the **Federal Public Prosecutor General** (Generalbundesanwalt) due to suspected membership in a **terrorist organization** (SS129a charges)
- **January 26, 2026**: The **Bundeskriminalamt (BKA)** offered a **EUR 1,000,000 reward** for information leading to identification of suspects (deadline: February 24, 2026)
- Police launched a publicity campaign with leaflets and subway posters
- Interior Minister **Alexander Dobrindt** called it terrorism that "endangers lives"
- Berlin Mayor **Kai Wegner** explicitly labeled the attack **terrorism**
- New critical infrastructure protection legislation was fast-tracked through the Bundestag
- Security agencies were ordered to be "significantly reinforced" against left-wing extremism

## Vulkangruppe: Background

The **Vulkangruppe** (Volcano Group) is a far-left, eco-anarchist militant group that has conducted infrastructure sabotage operations in the Berlin-Brandenburg region since at least **2011**.

### Ideology

- Anti-capitalist, anti-fossil fuel, eco-anarchist
- Opposes what they term the "military-industrial complex"
- Insurrectionary anarchist tradition: non-hierarchical cell structure with independent attacks
- Has labeled Tesla CEO Elon Musk a "techno-fascist"
- Connects environmental destruction to colonialism and patriarchy

### Known Attacks

| Date | Target | Impact |
|------|--------|--------|
| October 2011 | Railway infrastructure (Berlin area) | Major rail disruptions; claimed as "Hekla-Empfangskomitee" |
| March 5, 2024 | **Electricity pylon near Tesla Gigafactory, Grunheide** | Factory halted for days; ~$1 billion in damages |
| September 9, 2025 | 110 kV pylons, Johannisthal (attributed, not formally claimed by Vulkangruppe) | 50,000 endpoints, 60-hour outage |
| January 3, 2026 | Cable bridge, Teltow Canal / Lichterfelde | 45,000 households, 4-5 day outage |

### Organizational Structure

German authorities report the group's "organisational structure, size, and membership demographics are unknown." They operate with **non-hierarchical structures** that privilege independent attacks and limit information known by each member -- consistent with insurrectionary anarchist operational security.

The group has been active for over a decade with **11 known arson attacks** but no arrests, suggesting sophisticated operational discipline.

## Infrastructure Vulnerability Implications

### Why Cable Bridges and Transition Pylons Are Vulnerable

Both attacks targeted **exposed, above-ground infrastructure junctions**:

1. **Transition pylons** (Johannisthal): where overhead lines meet underground cables -- a single point of failure
2. **Cable bridges** (Teltow Canal): concentrated cable runs crossing waterways in accessible, unprotected locations

These junctions are:
- **Physically accessible** with minimal security
- **Structurally concentrated** -- destroying one point severs multiple circuits
- **Slow to repair** -- heavy cable replacement requires civil engineering, not just electrical work
- **Cascading** -- losing transmission-level cables (110 kV) blacks out entire distribution networks downstream

### Systemic Risks Exposed

- **No redundancy for concentrated crossings**: both attacks succeeded because multiple critical cables shared a single physical path
- **60-hour to 5-day restoration times**: modern urban grids were not designed for rapid recovery from physical destruction of transmission infrastructure
- **Heating dependency**: in winter, electricity outages cascade into heating failures when CHP plants and electric heating are affected
- **Insider knowledge advantage**: the September attack reportedly required knowledge of grid topology that is not publicly obvious
- **Low-tech, high-impact**: simple incendiary attacks on exposed infrastructure caused city-scale disruptions lasting days

### Broader Context

The attacks raised concerns beyond left-wing extremism:
- German authorities noted the incidents exposed vulnerabilities that **state-level adversaries** (e.g., Russian sabotage operations) could exploit
- Germany's role as a NATO logistics hub and Ukraine supporter makes grid resilience a **national security** concern
- The incidents accelerated legislative action on critical infrastructure protection
